Prepare a Website

Maquettage

Before starting to code, a website goes through a few conception stages to identify what to go towards:

ZONING

Blocks to define where things go, roughly.
Overall layout of the website

WIREFRAME

Builds on ZONING
Adds placeholder text and images
Allows to give an idea of the VISUALS of the website

MOCKUP

Creates a rough version of the website, without code
Refines the decisions of previous steps
Inserts the assets of the project

MAQUETTE

Makes the mockup navigable
Gives an idea of the features and functions
Allows for rough testing of the links between pages

PROTOTYPE

a sort of v.00 of the website
CODED
For testing and adjusting to user feedback

Best Practices

Steps to rapidly conceive a good maquette:

  1. Create an interraction map, retracing a simulacra of a token user's experience, what will they look for, look at, click at...
  2. Don't be afraid to take it to paper to quickly freehand connections
  3. Focus on optimising the hierarchy of information FROM the mockup stage.

The Client's needs

How to define the client's need?
When faced with an indecisive uninspired client, there are a couple questions that can orient the discussion towards concrete decisions.

What is the website's purpose?

Is it a shop, a gallery, a forum? The website's type will dictate its contents, features, and other decisions.

What is the target audience of the website?

Age, gender, occupation...
Who uses the website influences how it's structured.
For exemple, a website for the elderly may feature bigger text, on for teenagers will have more trendy 'dynamic' design...

What services will the website provide?

A service likely means a FEATURE!
Highlight which ones are absolutely MANDATORY!
Point the client towards the right direction based on what type of site was identified in the first question.

Do you have references of other websites you like?

What are the things they like, the ones they want something similar to?
Failing to provide exemples themselves, they'll enjoy being provided with a portfolio of potential sites matching their needs, in different designs, to point out what they like and don't.

Do you already have an existing website?

If so, what do you want to keep from it, what do you want to change?

How often will this site need updates, and who will handle them?

This will inform how to structure the website to make it more or less easy to edit
Will it need an interface for the clientside?

How many interfaces do you want your website to display on?

AND which one's the most important? Laptop, mobile?

Does your company have a graphical chart to follow?

Colour schemes, fonts... even if only from a logo.
If not, what kind of aesthetics do you enjoy? Something more traditional, organic, modern...
Can link back to the exemples provided in question 4.

Budget and time of execution...

Simply asking the budget heads on can lead to undiplomatic situations...
Giving an estimate cost is already a better way to open that conversation, especially if already considering negociations, try offering a few different price-points of the website with reduction of some superfluous features, so the client can pick which is most adapted to their needs.

Ergonomy Rules

Things to keep in mind for the user's ease:

Clarity and space

Clogging the space will make the website unreadable, and muddle out all information.
Instead try to keep things minimal and spaced out, so the user's eye can roam without strain.
Outdated, redundant, or pointless contents should be removed to not overwhelm the user with too much at once.

Consistency

Keeping the different pages cohesive helps with clarity as well, as the user doesn't have to "relearn" where to find things each time.

Hierarchy

Use organised sizing to highlight important pieces of information and keep everything in a consistent hierarchy.

Contrast

Between sizes, colours, elements... Contrast drives the eye to important elements.

Fluid Navigation

Menues should be easely accessible at all times, that way the user can move between pages without having to first look for HOW to move between pages.
It also helps to provide the user with information of WHERE in the website they are currently, through titles and/or breadcrumb trails.

Time is of the essence...

Be it in load time, or navigation time! The user is impatient.
Keep the website light, optimise it and reduce bloat for the pages to load as fast as possible.
As mentioned before, navigation also needs to be quick, be it between pages or within one page.
When it comes to browsing, some refer to a 3 clicks rule, meaning that from the HOMEPAGE, it shouldn't take more than 3 clicks for the user to find what they're looking for.

Dynamism

A website with relevant animations will enhance the user experience and helps guiding them through.
BUT it's crucial to not overdo it, and lose your user to visual noise.
Dynamic actions also, importantly, include FEEDBACK, messages, or changes, that informs the user of wether or not their own actions worked, what happened etc... And reduce their confusion, doubt, stress...

The goal is to...

...limit the time during which the user is LOOKING for things instead of interracting with the website. It makes the website intuitive, and the content appear ATTRACTIVE.
